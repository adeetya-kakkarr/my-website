My name is Adeetya. I grew up in New Delhi, where I learned early that building your own income changes what's possible for you. I founded my first organization at 14 to give other young people access to entrepreneurship education. It was scrappy at first. Then it wasn't.

Along the way I ran youth policy for a state government, worked on environmental education with a tribal community in northeast India, and helped shift national legislation through an advocacy campaign that reached people in over 50 countries. Each role taught me something the last one didn't.

I moved to Toronto in 2025 on a full-ride scholarship to the Rotman School of Management at the University of Toronto, one of fewer than 30 awarded globally each year. I currently work as a strategy consultant helping students get into the world's top universities, and I have a paper coming out in an academic journal.

The thread through all of it: I care most about who doesn't have access, and what it would actually take to change that.

What I'm working toward

"Economic independence isn't just a financial concept. For a lot of people, it's the difference between being able to live honestly and not."

That's the question I keep returning to, especially for queer youth in countries where coming out carries a real financial risk. I was lucky enough to have a scholarship that meant I didn't have to depend on anyone who might withdraw support if I was honest about who I am. Most people don't have that. Long-term, I want to build something that shifts that calculus, creating economic pathways that make financial independence more accessible for people who need it most.

Research & writing.

Published Research
The Deinfluencing Paradox: Digital Anti-Consumption Communities & Commodification of Resistance in Late Capitalism
Undergraduate Sociology Journal • Laidlaw Scholars Programme, UofT

What the paper asks

What happens when anti-consumerism becomes a brand strategy?

There's a growing corner of the internet built around rejecting consumerism: communities dedicated to buying less, calling out brands, resisting the always-on marketing machine.

This paper looks at what happens when the brands those communities are pushing against start using that same resistance as a marketing angle. At what point does opposition become content? And what does that mean for people who thought they were opting out?

Written through the Laidlaw Scholars Programme at Rotman, supervised by faculty at the Institute for Communication, Culture, Information and Technology.
